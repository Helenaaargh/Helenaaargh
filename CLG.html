Understanding HTML and CSS

What is HTML?

HTML stands for Hyper-Text Markup Language and is used to create webpages.

Imagine a web page as if it were a human body, and HTML is the skeleton inside it. Just like our bones give our bodies structure and shape, HTML gives a web page its structure and layout.

In this "web page body," the HTML skeleton consists of different parts, just like our skeleton has different bones. Each bone has a specific place and function. Similarly, in HTML, there are different elements, like headings, paragraphs, images, and links, each with its own job in making the web page

The skeleton or HTML is the bare bones of the webpage, and as such, it comes with a default font size, style and colour like the one you are seeing right now.

What does this structure look like?

  • HEAD: Contains the title, links, styles and scripts

    • Just like a skeleton needs a brain to control it, a webpage needs the "head" of HTML to provide important information, connect to other things, determine its appearance, and give it the ability to do special actions.

  • BODY: Contains the content, structure, links, buttons and lists

    • The "body" of HTML is like the visible part of your webpage, where you put all the text, images, and elements that people can see and interact with, just like the skin, organs, and structure of a skeleton make up the visible part of a person.


What's in the code

What is CSS?

CSS stands for Cascading Style Sheets and is a language used to style a webpage. Imagine a skeleton with its bones, but it looks plain and bare. CSS is what you use to give that skeleton a cool and stylish appearance:

  1. Colours: You can use CSS to change the color of the skeleton's bones and the clothes it wears. For example, you can make the bones white, the jacket black, and the shoes red.
  2. Size and Shape: CSS lets you adjust the size and shape of the skeleton's body parts. You can make its head bigger, its arms longer, or its legs thinner, just like tailoring clothes to fit perfectly.
  3. Position: You can position the skeleton's body parts in different ways. You might want to raise its arm, lower its leg, or tilt its head. CSS helps you control where each part goes, just like arranging clothes on a mannequin.
  4. Layout:CSS helps you arrange multiple skeletons (webpage elements) on a page. You can place them side by side, stack them, or create different layouts, just like arranging different mannequins in a store window.

CSS is like the fashion designer for a skeleton, making it look stylish and presentable (as you can see, I'm not much of a fashion designer), just like clothes and accessories do for a person. It gives your webpage its unique and attractive appearance.
